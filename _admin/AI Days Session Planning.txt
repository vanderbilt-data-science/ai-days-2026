AI Days Session Planning


Day 1: AI Literacy and Strategic Thinking for Faculty

9:00 AM — Introductions, Logistics (15 min)

9:15 AM — Live Podcast with Dean Matt Johnson-Roberson (45 min)

10:15 AM — How LLMs Actually Work: What Researchers Need to Know (60 min); Jesse Drawn from Course 1: Foundations of Generative AI Topics: Transformer architectures, reasoning models, capabilities and limitations. Pitched not as a CS lecture but as the minimum mental model researchers need to evaluate when AI is useful for their work and when vendor/tool claims are overblown. Faculty leave with a calibrated sense of what current models can and can't do, which matters for both using AI in their research and reviewing others' AI-assisted work. Compare and contrast custom ChatGPT vs Agent

11:30 AM — The Psychology of Human-AI Collaboration (30 min); Jesse Drawn from Course 2: AI Strategy and Organizational Dynamics Topics: Cognitive psychology of human-AI interaction, attention limitations, automation bias, decision biases, knowing when to abandon AI-assisted paths. This is the "critical thinking" workshop — particularly important for faculty who will be reviewing AI-assisted student work, incorporating AI into research pipelines, or advising students on appropriate use. Addresses the failure pattern of over-trusting plausible-sounding outputs.

12:00 PM — Agents in Action: Beyond ChatGPT (30 min); Jesse organizes, All Topics: Introduction to the agentic workflow, skills, and their utility in research and academics. Claude Code vs Claude Cowork, Codex. University can turn on Codex for some people. Level 2 not Level 3?! Research Skill for finding accessible housing, Knowledge trees, medical scheduling; Med School Year 1; Soccer?

12:30 PM — Agent Skills: Encapsulated Expertise (60 min); Jesse

2:30 PM — Defining AI-Augmented Academic Projects: When AI Helps and When It Doesn't (60 min); Abbie Drawn from Course 3: Problem Definition, Business Case, and Responsible Development Topics: Problem decomposition, scope definition, determining when AI is not appropriate, failure mode identification, metrics and baseline establishment. Faculty work through a structured framework for evaluating whether a research question or teaching challenge is actually a good candidate for AI augmentation. Counterbalances hype by giving people a disciplined "should we?" process before "how do we?"

Day 2: Building AI into Your Research and Teaching Practice

9:00 AM — Inside Agentic AI: Architectures, Workflows, and What's Real (60 min); Charreau Drawn from Course 1: Foundations of Generative AI and Course 4 Agentic Solution Development and Deployment Topics: Agentic architectures and workflows, subagents, components of AI systems. Major components of AI systems and differentiators in systems when AI is useful for their work and when vendor/tool claims are overblown. Faculty leave with a calibrated sense of driving components of agentic systems behind the scenes, which matters for both using and evaluating AI in their research and reviewing others' AI-assisted work.

10:15 AM — Building AI Research Assistants with Agent Skills (75 min); Umang Drawn from Course 1: Foundations of Generative AI Topics: Agent Skills specification, Model Context Protocol, transforming general-purpose AI into specialized assistants. Hands-on session where faculty build a simple Agent Skill relevant to their domain — a literature review assistant, a methodology advisor, a data analysis helper. This is the session that moves from "using ChatGPT" to "building domain-specific AI tools," which is where the real research productivity gains are.

11:45 AM — IP, Bias, and Privacy: What to Know Before You Use AI in Research (60 min); Umang + Mark Williams Drawn from Course 3: Problem Definition, Business Case, and Responsible Development Topics: Intellectual property considerations for AI-generated content, bias identification and fairness assessment, privacy frameworks, human oversight requirements. Tailored to the academic context — authorship questions, data privacy in research, IRB considerations when AI is part of the methodology, fairness concerns in AI-assisted grading or admissions. This is the workshop faculty will want before putting AI into any formal research protocol.

1:45 PM — Specification-Driven Development: Building Research Tools Without Coding (90 min); Myranda Drawn from Course 4: Agentic Solution Development and Deployment Topics: Specification writing as primary development artifact, directing agentic coding tools, test-driven development with agents, evaluation of agent output. Faculty learn to write clear specifications and use AI coding agents functional tools — data processing scripts, visualization dashboards, analysis pipelines — without prior programming experience. The emphasis on specification writing over coding is what makes this accessible to non-CS faculty.

3:30 PM — Designing AI-Enhanced Courses: Organizational and Pedagogical Considerations (60 min); Jesse Drawn from Course 2: AI Strategy and Organizational Dynamics Topics: Stakeholder mapping, change management, capturing institutional knowledge, designing implementations that build buy-in. Reframed for the teaching context — how to redesign a course to meaningfully incorporate AI, how to bring along skeptical colleagues or departments, how to handle the student-facing challenges (academic integrity, equity of access, pedagogical value). Uses the four-frame organizational analysis applied to academic departments and curricula.

4:45 PM — Building Your Solution (90 min); Jesse, Charreau, Myranda Interactive workshop where build one solution, then assist in teams as participants build their own. Bring materials, build, share
