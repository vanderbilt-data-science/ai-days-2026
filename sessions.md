### How LLMs Actually Work: What Researchers Need to Know

**10:00 AM – 11:00 AM** (60 minutes)

**Presenter:** Jesse Spencer-Smith, Ph.D. — Interim Director and Chief Data Scientist, Vanderbilt Data Science Institute; Associate Dean for Partnerships and Innovation, College of Connected Computing; Professor of the Practice of Computer Science

Drawn from Course 1: Foundations of Generative AI. Topics: Transformer architectures, reasoning models, capabilities and limitations. Pitched not as a CS lecture but as the minimum mental model researchers need to evaluate when AI is useful for their work and when vendor/tool claims are overblown. Faculty leave with a calibrated sense of what current models can and can't do, which matters for both using AI in their research and reviewing others' AI-assisted work. Compare and contrast custom ChatGPT vs Agent.

### The Psychology of Human-AI Collaboration

**11:15 AM – 11:45 AM** (30 minutes)

**Presenter:** Jesse Spencer-Smith, Ph.D. — Interim Director and Chief Data Scientist, Vanderbilt Data Science Institute; Associate Dean for Partnerships and Innovation, College of Connected Computing; Professor of the Practice of Computer Science

Drawn from Course 2: AI Strategy and Organizational Dynamics. Topics: Cognitive psychology of human-AI interaction, attention limitations, automation bias, decision biases, knowing when to abandon AI-assisted paths. This is the "critical thinking" workshop — particularly important for faculty who will be reviewing AI-assisted student work, incorporating AI into research pipelines, or advising students on appropriate use. Addresses the failure pattern of over-trusting plausible-sounding outputs.

---

### Agents in Action: Beyond ChatGPT

**11:45 AM – 12:15 PM** (30 minutes)

**Presenter:** Jesse Spencer-Smith, Ph.D. — Interim Director and Chief Data Scientist, Vanderbilt Data Science Institute; Associate Dean for Partnerships and Innovation, College of Connected Computing; Professor of the Practice of Computer Science

Introduction to the agentic workflow, skills, and their utility in research and academics. Live demonstrations of Claude Code vs Claude Cowork, Codex, and other agentic tools. Examples include a research skill for finding accessible housing, knowledge trees, medical scheduling, and other domain-specific applications. Faculty see what "beyond ChatGPT" looks like in practice.

---

*12:15 PM – 1:15 PM — Lunch*

---

### Agent Skills: Encapsulated Expertise

**1:15 PM – 2:15 PM** (60 minutes)

**Presenter:** Jesse Spencer-Smith, Ph.D. — Interim Director and Chief Data Scientist, Vanderbilt Data Science Institute; Associate Dean for Partnerships and Innovation, College of Connected Computing; Professor of the Practice of Computer Science

A deep dive into Agent Skills — self-contained packages of domain expertise that transform general-purpose AI into specialized assistants. Participants learn how skills encapsulate knowledge, workflows, and best practices into reusable components that can be shared across teams and institutions. Bridges the gap between the morning's conceptual foundation and the afternoon's practical evaluation framework.

---

### Defining AI-Augmented Academic Projects: When AI Helps and When It Doesn't

**2:15 PM – 3:15 PM** (60 minutes)

**Presenter:** Abbie Petulante, Ph.D. — Data Scientist, Vanderbilt Data Science Institute

Drawn from Course 3: Problem Definition, Business Case, and Responsible Development. Topics: Problem decomposition, scope definition, determining when AI is not appropriate, failure mode identification, metrics and baseline establishment. Faculty work through a structured framework for evaluating whether a research question or teaching challenge is actually a good candidate for AI augmentation. Counterbalances hype by giving people a disciplined "should we?" process before "how do we?"

## Day 2 — Thursday, March 5: Building AI into Your Research and Teaching Practice

---

### Inside Agentic AI: Architectures, Workflows, and What's Real

**9:00 AM – 10:00 AM** (60 minutes)

**Presenter:** Charreau Bell, Ph.D. — Senior Data Scientist, Vanderbilt Data Science Institute; Research Assistant Professor of Computer Science; Faculty Director, Undergraduate Data Science Minor

Drawn from Course 1: Foundations of Generative AI and Course 4: Agentic Solution Development and Deployment. Topics: Agentic architectures and workflows, subagents, components of AI systems. Major components of AI systems and differentiators in systems when AI is useful for their work and when vendor/tool claims are overblown. Faculty leave with a calibrated sense of the driving components of agentic systems behind the scenes, which matters for both using and evaluating AI in their research and reviewing others' AI-assisted work.

### Building AI Research Assistants with Agent Skills

**10:10 AM – 11:25 AM** (75 minutes)

**Presenter:** Umang Chaudhry — Senior Data Scientist, Vanderbilt Data Science Institute

Drawn from Course 1: Foundations of Generative AI. Topics: Agent Skills specification, Model Context Protocol, transforming general-purpose AI into specialized assistants. Hands-on session where faculty build a simple Agent Skill relevant to their domain — a literature review assistant, a methodology advisor, a data analysis helper. This is the session that moves from "using ChatGPT" to "building domain-specific AI tools," which is where the real research productivity gains are.

### IP, Bias, and Privacy: What to Know Before You Use AI in Research

**11:40 AM – 12:40 PM** (60 minutes)

**Presenters:** Umang Chaudhry — Senior Data Scientist, Vanderbilt Data Science Institute; Mark Williams — VAILL Co-Director; Professor of the Practice of Law, Vanderbilt Law School

Drawn from Course 3: Problem Definition, Business Case, and Responsible Development. Topics: Intellectual property considerations for AI-generated content, bias identification and fairness assessment, privacy frameworks, human oversight requirements. Tailored to the academic context — authorship questions, data privacy in research, IRB considerations when AI is part of the methodology, fairness concerns in AI-assisted grading or admissions. This is the workshop faculty will want before putting AI into any formal research protocol.

### Specification-Driven Development: Building Research Tools Without Coding

**1:40 PM – 3:10 PM** (90 minutes)

**Presenter:** Myranda Shirk — Senior Data Scientist, Vanderbilt Data Science Institute

Drawn from Course 4: Agentic Solution Development and Deployment. Topics: Specification writing as primary development artifact, directing agentic coding tools, test-driven development with agents, evaluation of agent output. Faculty learn to write clear specifications and use AI coding agents to build functional tools — data processing scripts, visualization dashboards, analysis pipelines — without prior programming experience. The emphasis on specification writing over coding is what makes this accessible to non-CS faculty.

### Designing AI-Enhanced Courses: Organizational and Pedagogical Considerations

**3:25 PM – 4:25 PM** (60 minutes)

**Presenter:** Jesse Spencer-Smith, Ph.D. — Interim Director and Chief Data Scientist, Vanderbilt Data Science Institute; Associate Dean for Partnerships and Innovation, College of Connected Computing; Professor of the Practice of Computer Science

Drawn from Course 2: AI Strategy and Organizational Dynamics. Topics: Stakeholder mapping, change management, capturing institutional knowledge, designing implementations that build buy-in. ### Building Your Solution

**4:35 PM – 6:05 PM** (90 minutes)

**Presenters:** Jesse Spencer-Smith, Ph.D. — Interim Director and Chief Data Scientist, Vanderbilt Data Science Institute; Charreau Bell, Ph.D. — Senior Data Scientist, Vanderbilt Data Science Institute; Myranda Shirk — Senior Data Scientist, Vanderbilt Data Science Institute

Interactive capstone workshop. The instructional team builds one complete solution live, then assists participants as they work in teams to build their own. Bring your research questions, teaching challenges, or workflow problems — this is where concepts become working tools. Participants leave with a functional prototype and the skills to iterate on it independently.
